Phonology

Vowels

There are two sets of vowels: Up vowels and down vowels, divided into 5 groups (a, e, i, o, u). This distinction is used to harmonize particles.

Group a e i o u
Up a ɛ i ɔ ɯ
Down ə~ɐ e ɪ o u

In the romanized orthography, up vowels are preferably represented with an acute accent ◌́, but double vowels can be used instead.

Phonotactics

The syllable structure is (C)1V(C)2
For (C)1, all consonants are allowed. Similarly, for V, all vowels are allowed.
For (C)2, only the nasals, unvoiced plosives, approximants, l, ly and r are allowed.
Double consonants are allowed, where C2 is the same consonant as the next syllable's C1.

Lowercase letters are their specific IPA symbol. A space separates syllables.

In the romanization system, every syllable is divided with a space.

Orthography

Logography

Logograms also mostly represent individual words, but some morphemes that inflect verbs/adjectives will have two readings based on the vowel groups. Their default reading is the low one.

Styling: The characters are organized in square blocks. They consist entirely of straight (parallel to the X or Y axis) lines and diagonal lines, and compound lines, which can only be formed exclusively from straight or diagonal lines. In other words, straight and diagonal lines cannot combine.

Forming characters: The characters can be formed in one of three ways:

  • Pictographic: A picture or a representation of the idea.
  • Ideographic: Pictures joined together to form a broader idea.
  • Phonetic: A semantic component, giving an idea of meaning, and a phonetic component, with a similar sound.

Alphabet

Some of the simpler or simplified logograms are used also as an alphabet, to write loanwords and such phonetically, written with a line under.
